background-color: rgb(235,235,210)\"><a href=\"/wiki/Synonym_(taxonomy)\" title=\"Synonym (taxonomy)\">Synonyms</a>\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td colspan=\"2\" style=\"text-align: left\">\n<p><i>Colymbus arcticus</i> <span style=\"font-size:85%;\">Linnaeus,&#160;1758</span>\n</p>\n</td></tr>\n\n</tbody></table><style data-mw-deduplicate=\"TemplateStyles:r1238732961\">@media screen{html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota tr{background:transparent!important}html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota img{background:transparent}}@media screen and (prefers-color-scheme:dark){html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota tr{background:transparent!important}html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota img{background:white}}.mw-parser-output .infobox.biota .taxobox-edit-taxonomy img{background:transparent!important}body.skin-vector .mw-parser-output table.biota.infobox{margin-top:0.5em}body.skin--responsive .mw-parser-output table.biota.infobox tr.taxonrow td{padding:2px 10px}</style>\n<p>The <b>black-throated loon</b> (<i><b>Gavia arctica</b></i>), also known as the <b>Arctic loon</b> and the <b>black-throated diver</b>, is a <a href=\"/wiki/Bird_migration\" title=\"Bird migration\">migratory</a> aquatic bird found in the northern hemisphere, primarily breeding in freshwater lakes in northern Europe and Asia. It winters along sheltered, ice-free coasts of the north-east Atlantic Ocean and the eastern and western Pacific Ocean. This loon was first described by <a href=\"/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus\" title=\"Carl Linnaeus\">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1758 and has two subspecies. It was previously considered to be the same species as the <a href=\"/wiki/Pacific_loon\" title=\"Pacific loon\">Pacific loon</a>, of which it is traditionally considered to be a <a href=\"/wiki/Sister_species\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Sister species\">sister species</a>, although this is debated. In a study that used <a href=\"/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA\" title=\"Mitochondrial DNA\">mitochondrial</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Intron\" title=\"Intron\">nuclear intron DNA</a>, the black-throated loon was found to be sister to a <a href=\"/wiki/Clade\" title=\"Clade\">clade</a> consisting of the Pacific loon and two sister species, the <a href=\"/wiki/Common_loon\" title=\"Common loon\">common loon</a> and the <a href=\"/wiki/Yellow-billed_loon\" title=\"Yellow-billed loon\">yellow-billed loon</a>.\n</p><p>The black-throated loon measures about 70&#160;cm (28&#160;in) in length and can weigh anywhere from 1.3 to 3.4 kilograms (2.9 to 7.5&#160;lb). In breeding plumage, the adult of the nominate subspecies has mostly black upperparts, with the exception of some of the <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#mantle\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}mantle\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">mantle</span></a></dfn> and <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#scapulars\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}scapulars\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">scapulars</span></a></dfn>, which have white squares. The head and hindneck are grey, and the sides white and striped black. Most of the throat is also black, giving this bird the name \"black-throated loon\". The colour of the throat patch can be used to distinguish the two subspecies; the throat patch of the other subspecies, <i>G. a. viridigularis</i>, is green. The underparts are mostly white, including the bottom of the throat. The flanks are also white, a feature which can be used to separate this bird from the Pacific loon. When it is not breeding, the black patch on the throat is absent, replaced with white; most of the black lines on the throat are also missing, except those on the bottom sides, and the upperparts are unpatterned with the exception of a few white spots on the <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#upperwing\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}upperwing\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">upperwing</span></a></dfn>. The juvenile is similar to the non-breeding adult, except more brown overall.\n</p><p>The timing of the breeding season is variable; in the southern part of its range, this loon starts breeding in April, whereas in the northern portion, it waits until after the spring thaw. It builds an oval nest about 23 centimetres (9.1&#160;in) across, either near the breeding lake or on vegetation emerging from it. The black-throated loon usually lays a clutch of two, rarely one or three, brown-green eggs with dark splotches. After an incubation period of 27<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>to 29<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>days, the chick hatches, and is fed a diet of small fish and invertebrates. The adult mostly eats fish. To catch this food, it forages by itself or in pairs, very rarely foraging in groups. It dives from the water, going no deeper than 5 metres (16&#160;ft). Most dives are successful. Whether or not at least one chick will hatch from a nest is variable, ranging from 30% to 90%. Most failures come from predators and flooding. Overall, the population of this loon is declining, although the <a href=\"/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature\" title=\"International Union for Conservation of Nature\">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) still rates it as <a href=\"/wiki/Least_concern\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Least concern\">least concern</a>, because the population decline is not rapid enough. The black-throated loon is protected under both the <a href=\"/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act_of_1918\" title=\"Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918\">Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918</a> and the <a href=\"/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds\" title=\"Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds\">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a>.\n</p>\n<meta property=\"mw:PageProp/toc\" />\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Taxonomy_and_etymology\">Taxonomy and etymology</h2></div>\n<p>The black-throated loon was <a href=\"/wiki/Species_description\" title=\"Species description\">formally described</a> in 1758 by the Swedish naturalist <a href=\"/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus\" title=\"Carl Linnaeus\">Carl Linnaeus</a> in the <a href=\"/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae\" title=\"10th edition of Systema Naturae\">tenth edition</a> of his <i><a href=\"/wiki/Systema_Naturae\" title=\"Systema Naturae\">Systema Naturae</a></i> under the <a href=\"/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature\" title=\"Binomial nomenclature\">binomial name</a> <i>Colymbus arcticus</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-2\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>2<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Linnaeus specified the <a href=\"/wiki/Type_locality_(biology)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Type locality (biology)\">type locality</a> as Europe and America but in 1761 he restricted it to Sweden.<sup id=\"cite_ref-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-3\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>3<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-4\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-4\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>4<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1897 the American ornithologist <a href=\"/wiki/Joel_Asaph_Allen\" title=\"Joel Asaph Allen\">Joel Asaph Allen</a> suggested that the  black-throated loon should be moved to the genus <i><a href=\"/wiki/Gavia_(bird)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Gavia (bird)\"> Gavia</a></i> that had been introduced by <a href=\"/wiki/Johann_Reinhold_Forster\" title=\"Johann Reinhold Forster\">Johann Forster</a> in 1788. This proposal was adopted by the <a href=\"/wiki/American_Ornithologists%27_Union\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"American Ornithologists&#39; Union\">American Ornithologists' Union</a> (AOU) in 1899.<sup id=\"cite_ref-5\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-5\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>5<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-6\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-6\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>6<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The genus name <i>Gavia</i> comes from the <a href=\"/wiki/Latin\" title=\"Latin\">Latin</a> for \"sea mew\", as used by the ancient Roman naturalist <a href=\"/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder\" title=\"Pliny the Elder\">Pliny the Elder</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Johnsgard_7-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Johnsgard-7\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>7<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The specific <i>arctica</i> is Latin for \"northern\" or \"Arctic\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-job90_8-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-job90-8\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>8<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The name of the subspecies <i>viridigularis</i> stems from the <a href=\"/wiki/Latin\" title=\"Latin\">Latin</a> \"viridis\", meaning \"green\", and the Latin \"gularis\", meaning \"throated\", in reference to the green throat of this subspecies.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbwname_9-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbwname-9\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>9<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The common name, black-throated loon, stems from its black throat patch. This loon is also called the Arctic loon and the black-throated diver.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>There are two <a href=\"/wiki/Subspecies\" title=\"Subspecies\">subspecies</a>:<sup id=\"cite_ref-ioc_11-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-ioc-11\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>11<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Gavia arctica arctica</i> <small>(Linnaeus, 1758)</small> – This subspecies is found in northern Europe, east to the center of <a href=\"/wiki/Palearctic\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Palearctic\">northern Asia</a>, and from that to the <a href=\"/wiki/Lena_River\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Lena River\">Lena River</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Transbaikal\" title=\"Transbaikal\">Transbaikal</a>. It migrates to the coasts of northwestern Europe and the coasts of the Mediterranean, Black, and Caspian Seas.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>\n<li><i>G. a. viridigularis</i> <small><a href=\"/wiki/Jonathan_Dwight\" title=\"Jonathan Dwight\">Dwight</a>, 1918</small> – This subspecies is found in eastern Russia from the Lena River and Transbaikal east to the peninsulas of <a href=\"/wiki/Chukchi_Peninsula\" title=\"Chukchi Peninsula\">Chukotka</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula\" title=\"Kamchatka Peninsula\">Kamchatka</a> and the northern portion of <a href=\"/wiki/Sakhalin\" title=\"Sakhalin\">Sakhalin</a>. It migrates to the northwestern Pacific coasts.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>\n<p><i>G. a. viridigularis</i> was considered to be a separate species when described by <a href=\"/wiki/Jonathan_Dwight\" title=\"Jonathan Dwight\">Jonathan Dwight</a> in 1918,<sup id=\"cite_ref-Dwight1918_12-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Dwight1918-12\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>12<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> but in 1919 <a href=\"/wiki/Arthur_Cleveland_Bent\" title=\"Arthur Cleveland Bent\">Arthur Cleveland Bent</a> suggested that it be moved to its current placement as a subspecies.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Bent1919_13-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Bent1919-13\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>13<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The black-throated loon was previously considered conspecific with the North American <a href=\"/wiki/Pacific_loon\" title=\"Pacific loon\">Pacific loon</a>, which was its subspecies,<sup id=\"cite_ref-AOU_14-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-AOU-14\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>14<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> but they have now been <a href=\"/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters\" title=\"Lumpers and splitters\">split</a> into two species;<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> there was no evidence of the two interbreeding in areas where they occurred together.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sprengelmeyer2014_15-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sprengelmeyer2014-15\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>15<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, the architecture of the air sacs in the lungs of the two species are significantly different.<sup id=\"cite_ref-16\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-16\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>16<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> This split was done by the AOU in 1985.<sup id=\"cite_ref-AOU_14-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-AOU-14\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>14<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The phylogeny of this species is debated, the black-throated loon and the Pacific loon traditionally being considered <a href=\"/wiki/Sister_species\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Sister species\">sister species</a>, whereas a study using <a href=\"/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA\" title=\"Mitochondrial DNA\">mitochondrial</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Intron\" title=\"Intron\">nuclear intron DNA</a> supported placing the black-throated loon sister to a <a href=\"/wiki/Clade\" title=\"Clade\">clade</a> consisting of the Pacific loon and the two sister species that are the <a href=\"/wiki/Common_loon\" title=\"Common loon\">common loon</a> and the <a href=\"/wiki/Yellow-billed_loon\" title=\"Yellow-billed loon\">yellow-billed loon</a>. This latter study is criticised on the basis that it may form a phylogeny on <a href=\"/wiki/Incomplete_lineage_sorting\" title=\"Incomplete lineage sorting\">incomplete lineage sorting</a>. In the former phylogeny, the split between the Pacific loon and the black-throated loon is proposed to have happened about 6.5 million years ago.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sprengelmeyer2014_15-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sprengelmeyer2014-15\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>15<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Description\">Description</h2></div>\n<p>The adult black-throated loon is 58 to 73&#160;cm (23 to 29&#160;in) in length with a 100 to 130&#160;cm (39 to 51&#160;in) wingspan and a weight of 1.3 to 3.4 kilograms (2.9 to 7.5&#160;lb). The <a href=\"/wiki/Nominate_subspecies\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Nominate subspecies\">nominate subspecies</a> in its <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#breeding_plumage\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}breeding plumage\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">breeding plumage</span></a></dfn> has a grey head and hindneck, with a black throat and a large black patch on the foreneck, both of which have a soft purple gloss. The lower throat has a necklace-shaped patch of short parallel white lines. The sides of the throat have about five long parallel white lines that start at the side of the patch on the lower throat and run down to the chest, which also has a pattern of parallel white and black lines. The rest of the <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#underparts\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}underparts\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">underparts</span></a></dfn>, including the centre of the chest, are pure white. The upperparts are blackish down to the base of the wing, where there are a few rows of high contrast white squares that cover the <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#mantle\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}mantle\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">mantle</span></a></dfn> and <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#scapulars\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}scapulars\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">scapulars</span></a></dfn>. There are small white spots on both the lesser and median coverts. The rest of the <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#upperwing\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}upperwing\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">upperwing</span></a></dfn> is a blackish colour. The <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#underwing\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}underwing\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">underwing</span></a></dfn> is paler than the upperwing, and the underwing coverts are white. The tail is blackish. The bill and legs are black, with a pale grey colour on the inner half of the legs. The toes and the webs are grey, the latter also being flesh coloured. The irides are a deep brown-red. The sexes are alike, and the subspecies <i>viridigularis</i> is very similar to the nominate except that the former has a green throat patch, instead of black.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-4\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The subspecies <i>viridigularis</i> retains a purplish gloss, although less than the nominate.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Bent1919_13-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Bent1919-13\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>13<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class=\"mw-default-size mw-halign-right\" typeof=\"mw:File/Thumb\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:GaviaArctica.svg\" class=\"mw-file-description\"><img src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/GaviaArctica.svg/220px-GaviaArctica.svg.png\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"106\" class=\"mw-file-element\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/GaviaArctica.svg/330px-GaviaArctica.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/GaviaArctica.svg/440px-GaviaArctica.svg.png 2x\" data-file-width=\"410\" data-file-height=\"198\" /></a><figcaption>Breeding, top, and non-breeding, bottom, black-throated loons</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The non-breeding adult differs from the breeding adult in that the cap and the back of the neck are more brownish. The non-breeding adult also lacks the patterned upperparts of the breeding adult, although some of the upperwing coverts do not lose their white spots. This results in the upperparts being an almost unpatterned black from above. The sides of the throat are usually darker at the white border separating the sides of the throat and the front of the throat; most of the time a thin dark necklace between these two areas can be seen. There is white on the sides of the head that are below the eye. The bill is a steel-grey with, similar to the breeding adult, a blackish tip.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-5\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class=\"mw-default-size mw-halign-left\" typeof=\"mw:File/Thumb\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG\" class=\"mw-file-description\"><img src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG/220px-Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"167\" class=\"mw-file-element\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG/330px-Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG/440px-Gavia_arcticaLake_Zeller_02.JPG 2x\" data-file-width=\"3505\" data-file-height=\"2654\" /></a><figcaption>Non-breeding adult</figcaption></figure>\n<p>The juvenile is similar to the non-breeding adult, but has a browner appearance. It has a buffy scaling on the upperparts that is especially pronounced on the scapulars. The lower face and front of the neck has a diffused brownish tinge. The juvenile does not have the white spots on the wing coverts, and its irides are darker and more dull in colour. The chick hatches with <a href=\"/wiki/Down_feather\" title=\"Down feather\">down feathers</a> that range from sooty-brown to brownish-grey, usually with a slightly paler head. The abdomen is pale.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-6\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The black-throated loon can be distinguished from the Pacific loon by the white on the flanks of the former.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Floyd2008_17-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Floyd2008-17\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>17<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading3\"><h3 id=\"Vocalizations\">Vocalizations</h3></div>\n<p>The male, when breeding, vocalizes a loud and rhythmic \"oooéé-cu-cloooéé-cu-cloooéé-cu-cluuéé\" whistling song. A \"áááh-oo\" wail can also be heard, and a growling or croaking \"knarr-knor\", a sound given especially at night. The alarm call at the nest is a rising \"uweek\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-7\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Distribution_and_habitat\">Distribution and habitat</h2></div>\n<p>The black-throated loon has a large range, breeding taking place across northern Europe, Asia,<sup id=\"cite_ref-iucn_status_13_November_2021_1-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-iucn_status_13_November_2021-1\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>1<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the <a href=\"/wiki/Seward_Peninsula\" title=\"Seward Peninsula\">Seward Peninsula</a> in Alaska.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Floyd2008_17-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Floyd2008-17\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>17<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> When breeding, it is found in the area around isolated, deep freshwater lakes<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hauber2014_18-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hauber2014-18\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>18<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> larger than 0.1 square kilometres (0.039&#160;sq&#160;mi),<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hakeetal.2005_19-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hakeetal.2005-19\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>19<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> especially those with inlets,<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-8\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> as it prefers to face only small stretches of open water.<sup id=\"cite_ref-MudgeTalbot1992_20-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-MudgeTalbot1992-20\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>20<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> When it is not breeding, this loon moves in a general southward direction and towards ice-free sea,<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-9\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> usually wintering in coasts on north-east Atlantic Ocean and those on the eastern and western Pacific Ocean, such as the coasts of Japan.<sup id=\"cite_ref-iucn_status_13_November_2021_1-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-iucn_status_13_November_2021-1\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>1<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> During this time, its habitat is usually inshore waters along sheltered coasts, although it will sometimes be found inland, in places such as the Mediterranean and Black seas.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-10\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Behaviour\">Behaviour</h2></div>\n<figure class=\"mw-default-size\" typeof=\"mw:File/Thumb\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg\" class=\"mw-file-description\"><img src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg/220px-Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"124\" class=\"mw-file-element\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg/330px-Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg/440px-Gavia_arctica_taking_off.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"2174\" data-file-height=\"1225\" /></a><figcaption>A black-throated loon taking off</figcaption></figure>\n<p>Like other loons, this bird takes off by pattering on a \"runway\" of water.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Pielou2012_21-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Pielou2012-21\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>21<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> While flying, it makes a barking \"kwow\" <a href=\"/wiki/Flight_call\" title=\"Flight call\">flight call</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-PetersonMountfort2001_22-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-PetersonMountfort2001-22\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>22<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading3\"><h3 id=\"Breeding\">Breeding</h3></div>\n<figure class=\"mw-default-size\" typeof=\"mw:File/Thumb\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Black_throated_loon_family_feeding_on_Lake_Str%C3%A4ken,_Sweden.jpg\" class=\"mw-file-description\"><img src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Black_throated_loon_family_feeding_on_Lake_Str%C3%A4ken%2C_Sweden.jpg/220px-Black_throated_loon_family_feeding_on_Lake_Str%C3%A4ken%2C_Sweden.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"42\" class=\"mw-file-element\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Black_throated_loon_family_feeding_on_Lake_Str%C3%A4ken%2C_Sweden.jpg/330px-Black_throated_loon_family_feeding_on_Lake_Str%C3%A4ken%2C_Sweden.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"2600\" data-file-height=\"500\" /></a><figcaption>Black-throated loon family feeding on <a href=\"https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Str%C3%A5ken_(Mullsj%C3%B6)\" class=\"extiw\" title=\"sv:Stråken (Mullsjö)\">Lake Stråken</a>, Sweden</figcaption></figure>\n<p>This species usually nests on the ground<sup id=\"cite_ref-Petersen1979_23-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Petersen1979-23\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>23<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> within about 1 metre (3.3&#160;ft) of the lake it breeds at.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hakeetal.2005_19-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hakeetal.2005-19\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>19<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> This loon also sometimes nests on vegetation, like <i><a href=\"/wiki/Arctophila_fulva\" title=\"Arctophila fulva\">Arctophila fulva</a></i>, that has emerged from lakes. The nest site is often reused the next year.<sup id=\"cite_ref-BergmanDerksen1977_24-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BergmanDerksen1977-24\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>24<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The nest itself is oval-shaped<sup id=\"cite_ref-Petersen1979_23-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Petersen1979-23\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>23<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> and built mostly by the female<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sjolander1978_25-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sjolander1978-25\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>25<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> out of heaped plant material like leaves and sticks.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hauber2014_18-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hauber2014-18\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>18<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The nest is about 23 centimetres (9.1&#160;in) across. Families of black-throated loons often move their nest site from the original nest ponds they inhabited to wetlands nearby after the chicks reach two weeks of age. The journey is generally less than 150 metres (490&#160;ft).<sup id=\"cite_ref-BergmanDerksen1977_24-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BergmanDerksen1977-24\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>24<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>In the southern portion of its range, this loon starts to breed in April, whereas in the northern parts of its range, it waits until the spring thaw,<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-11\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> when there is adequate water for it to take off.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Petersen1979_23-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Petersen1979-23\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>23<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> It usually arrives before the lake thaws, in the latter case.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sjolander1978_25-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sjolander1978-25\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>25<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Before copulation, the female hunches its neck and swims close to the shore until it finds a suitable place and then lies down on the shore. The male sometimes adopts the same posture as the female. During this time, the only vocalization made is a one note \"hum\". During copulation, the male, coming ashore, mounts the female and occasionally flaps its wings loudly. After this, the male returns to the water and <dfn id=\"\"><a href=\"/wiki/Glossary_of_bird_terms#preens\" title=\"Glossary of bird terms\"><span title=\"See entry at: Glossary of bird terms §\u{2006}preens\" style=\"color:inherit;\" class=\"glossary-link\">preens</span></a></dfn> itself. The female stays ashore for a maximum of about 23 minutes and usually starts to build the nest.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sjolander1978_25-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sjolander1978-25\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>25<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<figure class=\"mw-default-size\" typeof=\"mw:File/Thumb\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG\" class=\"mw-file-description\"><img src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG/220px-Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"213\" class=\"mw-file-element\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG/330px-Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG/440px-Gavia_arctica_MWNH_1918.JPG 2x\" data-file-width=\"2900\" data-file-height=\"2812\" /></a><figcaption>Eggs, Collection <a href=\"/wiki/Museum_Wiesbaden,_Germany\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Museum Wiesbaden, Germany\">Museum Wiesbaden, Germany</a></figcaption></figure>\n<p>The black-throated loon lays a clutch of two, very rarely one or three,<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-12\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> 76 by 47 millimetres (3.0 by 1.9&#160;in) eggs that are brown-green with darker speckles. These eggs are incubated by both parents for a period of 27<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>to 29<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>days,<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hauber2014_18-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hauber2014-18\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>18<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> with the female spending the most time incubating. During incubation, this bird turns its eggs. The interval between when they are turned is very irregular, ranging from one minute to about six hours.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sjolander1978_25-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sjolander1978-25\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>25<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> After they hatch, the mobile young are fed by both parents for a period of weeks.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hauber2014_18-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hauber2014-18\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>18<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The chicks fledge about 60<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>to 65<span class=\"nowrap\">&#160;</span>days after hatching, and achieve sexual maturity after two to three years.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-13\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>Nesting success, whether or not at least one chick will hatch from any given nest, is variable year to year, ranging from under 30% to over 90%.<sup id=\"cite_ref-BergmanDerksen1977_24-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BergmanDerksen1977-24\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>24<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> For clutches of two eggs, the average nesting success is about 50%, whereas in clutches with only one egg, this rate is about 60%.<sup id=\"cite_ref-MudgeTalbot1992_20-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-MudgeTalbot1992-20\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>20<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The nesting success is influenced most by predation,<sup id=\"cite_ref-Petersen1979_23-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Petersen1979-23\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>23<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> and flooding.<sup id=\"cite_ref-MudgeTalbot1992_20-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-MudgeTalbot1992-20\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>20<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some of the adults that lose their clutch early in the incubation period renest. Most of the time, only one chick survives to fledge, the other dying within seven days of hatching.<sup id=\"cite_ref-BergmanDerksen1977_24-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BergmanDerksen1977-24\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>24<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Scotland, a study concluded that a single pair usually fledges a chick, on average, 25% of the time per year.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-14\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> This can be increased by artificial means, such as constructing rafts for loons to nest on.<sup id=\"cite_ref-26\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-26\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>26<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Whether or not there is at least one chick fledged is influenced by the density of fish in the breeding lake; a lake with a higher density of fish usually reduces the chance that a pair will fledge a chick, even though this loon feeds mainly on fish. There are two factors that might contribute to this; the first being that aquatic insects, an alternative food source for chicks, are more dense when there are less fish, and the second being that a higher density of fish means more <a href=\"/wiki/Northern_pike\" title=\"Northern pike\">northern pike</a>, a predator of small chicks.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Eriksson1986_27-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Eriksson1986-27\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>27<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading3\"><h3 id=\"Feeding\">Feeding</h3></div>\n<p>A top predator in the <a href=\"/wiki/Pelagic_zone\" title=\"Pelagic zone\">pelagic zone</a> of some subarctic lakes,<sup id=\"cite_ref-AmundsenLafferty2009_28-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-AmundsenLafferty2009-28\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>28<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> this bird feeds on fish and sometimes insects, <a href=\"/wiki/Mollusc\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mollusc\">molluscs</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Crustacean\" title=\"Crustacean\">crustaceans</a>, and plant matter.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-15\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The black-throated loon usually forages by itself or in pairs, rarely feeding in groups with multiple species.<sup id=\"cite_ref-BaltzMorejohn1977_29-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BaltzMorejohn1977-29\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>29<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> It dives from the water,<sup id=\"cite_ref-De_GraafTilghman1985_30-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-De_GraafTilghman1985-30\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>30<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> at depths of no more than 5 metres (16&#160;ft).<sup id=\"cite_ref-Bundy2009_31-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Bundy2009-31\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>31<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Just before diving, this loon stretches and holds up its neck until it is erect and at full length. It usually jumps slightly upwards before diving.<sup id=\"cite_ref-BNA_32-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-BNA-32\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>32<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> These dives are frequent, with an average of about 1.6 dives per minute. Most dives, about 80% of them, are successful, and those that are successful are usually shorter than those that are unsuccessful, with an average of 17 seconds for each successful dive, and 27 seconds for each unsuccessful dive. When foraging for newly hatched chicks, the adult forages in the lake where the nest is or in nearby lakes, returning after a prey item has been caught. When the chicks are older, they usually accompany both of the parents, swimming a few metres behind them. The strategy that predominates immediately after hatching is generally still employed when the chicks are older, but at a reduced rate.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Jackson2002_33-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Jackson2002-33\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>33<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> The chicks are fed only one item of prey at a time. The young are also able to capture food themselves at least 36 days after hatching, although they are still fed daily up until about 70 days of age.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Sjolander1978_25-4\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Sjolander1978-25\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>25<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p><p>The diet of black-throated loon chicks varies, the prey in the breeding lake being a major factor. For the first eight days, chicks are usually fed <a href=\"/wiki/Three-spined_stickleback\" title=\"Three-spined stickleback\">three-spined sticklebacks</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Common_minnow\" title=\"Common minnow\">common minnows</a> if they are found in the breeding lake. If they are not present, then the chicks are brought up mainly on small invertebrates until about eight days, when they are able to take <a href=\"/wiki/Trout\" title=\"Trout\">trout</a> of about 100 millimetres (3.9&#160;in) in length. Although in these chicks trout makes up the majority of their diet, they are still fed invertebrates in large numbers. In all lakes, <a href=\"/wiki/Salmonid\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Salmonid\">salmonids</a> make up an important part of the chicks' diet after eight days. Salmonids, especially those between 100 and 240 millimetres (3.9 and 9.4&#160;in), are important in the diets of older chicks. Eels are also an important food for older chicks.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Jackson2002_33-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Jackson2002-33\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>33<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Predators_and_parasites\">Predators and parasites</h2></div>\n<p>The black-throated loon is sometimes parasitized by <i><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Eustrongylides_tubifex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1\" class=\"new\" title=\"Eustrongylides tubifex (page does not exist)\">Eustrongylides tubifex</a></i>, a species of <a href=\"/wiki/Nematode\" title=\"Nematode\">nematode</a> that can cause <a href=\"/wiki/Eustrongylidosis\" title=\"Eustrongylidosis\">Eustrongylidosis</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-FastzkieCrites1977_34-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-FastzkieCrites1977-34\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>34<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> Mammalian predators, such as <a href=\"/wiki/Red_fox\" title=\"Red fox\">red foxes</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Pine_marten\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Pine marten\">pine martens</a>,<sup id=\"cite_ref-Hakeetal.2005_19-2\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Hakeetal.2005-19\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>19<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> are likely the cause of about 40% of clutch losses. Avian predators, such as <a href=\"/wiki/Hooded_crow\" title=\"Hooded crow\">hooded crows</a>, also take the eggs of this loon.<sup id=\"cite_ref-MudgeTalbot1992_20-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-MudgeTalbot1992-20\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>20<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"Status\">Status</h2></div>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading3\"><h3 id=\"Conservation\">Conservation</h3></div>\n<p>Despite the fact that its population is declining, the black-throated loon is listed as a species of <a href=\"/wiki/Least_concern\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Least concern\">least concern</a> by the <a href=\"/wiki/IUCN\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"IUCN\">IUCN</a>. This is because the species has a large population and an extremely large range, and its decline does not appear to be rapid.<sup id=\"cite_ref-iucn_status_13_November_2021_1-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-iucn_status_13_November_2021-1\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>1<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the United States, it is protected under the <a href=\"/wiki/Migratory_Bird_Treaty_Act_of_1918\" title=\"Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918\">Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918</a>,<sup id=\"cite_ref-35\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-35\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>35<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup> while in Europe and Africa, it is protected under the <a href=\"/wiki/Agreement_on_the_Conservation_of_African-Eurasian_Migratory_Waterbirds\" title=\"Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds\">Agreement on the Conservation of African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbirds</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-36\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-36\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>36<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading3\"><h3 id=\"Threats\">Threats</h3></div>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Freshwater_acidification\" title=\"Freshwater acidification\">Acidification</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Toxic_heavy_metal\" title=\"Toxic heavy metal\">heavy-metal pollution</a> of the breeding lake possibly threatens this bird. It is also vulnerable to oil pollution, especially when near fishing grounds. Fishing nets are also a cause of mortality. This loon is sensitive to <a href=\"/wiki/Windfarm\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Windfarm\">windfarms</a> near the coast. Overall, the annual mortality rate of the adult black-throated loon is 10%.<sup id=\"cite_ref-hbw_10-16\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-hbw-10\"><span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#91;</span>10<span class=\"cite-bracket\">&#93;</span></a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"mw-heading mw-heading2\"><h2 id=\"References\">References</h2></div>\n<div
